Sticking to mainstream apps (consult with an Advocate if you're unsure) and not installing odd, unknown, apps or apps that aren't in your platform's official app store adds risk.

Email: Never Click Anything

There are exceptions, but if you start with the rule that you never click (or tap) on any email (or text or instant message) then you'll be a lot safer.

Malware

Email: Never Click Anything

Example: if you get an email from your bank and you need to take an action, then:

  • Open a browser tab, type in the web address, or...
  • Pick up your phone and call the bank (using a known-good number), or...
  • Drive to a branch and conduct your business in person.

Malware

Email: What Can I Click

Only emails that are sent in response to a very specific action you initiated, and where you are specifically expecting the email (and it comes in a very short time frame) and where you must complete the action by clicking a link in the email.

Malware

Email: What Can I Click

The only real-world example I can think of where I have had to click on an email button or link, is when I've signed up for a new online account somewhere and given them my email address and where they require me to "verify the email" address by clicking the link they email to me.

Malware

Backup Your Data

Many scammers try to steal your money directly, or infect your computer to steal your data (or attack other people you know or strangers, using your computer).

Sometimes, hackers delete or encrypt your data to charge you a ransom to get it back. To prevent this, back up your data.

Domain Names & Email Addresses

Why?

Understanding these can help you avoid a LOT of problems, detecting scam emails, bad web links, and knowing if the site you're visiting is likely legitimate or not.

Domain Names: DNS

Domain Names are part of the Domain Name System (DNS). These help us know how to access well-known websites. For instance, google.com is a "domain name".

Domain Names: DNS

A domain name is used to find the true, underlying "IP Address" of a given website: the actual address of a computer on the internet.

Domain Names: Hierarchy

Domain Names are like upside-down trees. There are roots and branches. But, being upside-down, the "roots" are referred to as the "top".

A name with no dots is a "top-level" (or root) domain name. For example, com, net, org and gov are common US top-level names.

Domain Names: Hierarchy

Top-level (or "root") domain names are never used directly on their own. There's always at least one more level (or branch) added. Different levels of the name are separated by dots: "."

A "second-level domain" would be google.com. The "com" is the top-level. The second-level is "google". They're put together with a dot.

Domain Names: Hierarchy

So, your read a domain-name from root (or "top") to leaf (or "bottom") going right-to-left.

leaf.branch.trunk.us

In the example above, this name as 4 levels. The top-level "domain" is "us". The next-level name is "trunk". Beneath that is "branch". The lowest level is "leaf".

Domain Names: Hierarchy

Ownership and control of names follows the hierarchy.

leaf.branch.trunk.us

The US government has authority over the "us" top-level domain name. They then delegate to some sub-organization for next-level names. Those organizations can in turn delegate control of lower-level names.

Domain Names: Hierarchy

Many companies register a "dot com" through a "registrar" in order to get control of a name from the international organization that controls the "com" TLD.

Then, within their own company, they may create "sub-domains" like "www" for "www.google.com" (www standing for "world wide web") or "gemini.google.com" where Google hosts their main AI tool Gemini's website.

Domain Names: Validity

The key is, the level of trust when reading a link, is based on reading the domain name from right-to-left.

bankofamerica.finances.com

The above looks like a scam. If this were really a Bank of American name, it'd be

finances.bankofamerica.com

Domain Names: Typo-Squatting

Beware names that look almost correct, but aren't:

  1. Omission: gogle.com vs. google.com
  2. Duplication: faacebook.com vs. fakebook.com
  3. Transposition: amzaon.com vs. amazon.com
  4. Strip-Casing: spIunk.com vs. splunk.com

Domain Names: Typo-Squatting

Beware names that look almost correct, but aren't:

  1. RN vs. M: rnicrosoft.com vs. microsoft.com
  2. TLD Typo: twitter.cm vs. twitter.com
  3. IDN Homographs: xn--80ak6aa92e.com (but looks like apple.com in vulnerable browsers) vs. apple.com (real 2017 example)

Domain Names: Typo-Squatting

Beware names that look almost correct, but aren't:

  1. Combosquatting (The Legitimacy Lure):
    1. paypal-secure.com
    2. amazon-support-login.com
    3. or any [brand]-[keyword].com
  2. TLD Confusion (The File Extension Trick): financial-report.zip

Domain Names: Within Links

Most links include several parts.

  1. Protocol: https, http, etc., followed by ://
  2. Username & Password (rare): one or both can be included before the domain name, followed by an "@" sign
  3. Domain Name
  4. Port (uncommon): A colon after the domain name followed by digits
  5. Path: a slash followed by lots of other characters, often with more slashes
  6. Query String: question mark followed by lots of stuff
  7. Fragment: pound sign followed by a few more characters
Example: https://username:password@google.com:443/path/to/file?query=string#fragment

Domain Names: Within Links

Some realistic link examples:

  1. https://mail.google.com/mail/u/0/#inbox
  2. http://localhost:8000/presentation.html#/9/3
  3. http://192.168.1.0/router/login
  4. https://zacpp.github.io/secure-your-life/presentation.html?view=scroll
